  <div class="sub-heading">The REER problem</div>
  <p>The nominal USD/PKR rate at 279 suggests stability. But Pakistan's <Term k="reer">Real Effective Exchange Rate</Term> tells a different story. The REER index climbed to 105.17 in March 2026 — its highest since September 2018, up 2% month-on-month and 3.5% year-on-year. A REER above 100 means Pakistan's goods are relatively more expensive than its trading partners' once you adjust for inflation differences; at 105.17 the rupee is elevated in real terms by roughly 5%.</p>

  <p>What this means practically: even though the nominal rate is flat, exporters lose competitiveness every month domestic inflation (10.9%) outpaces trading-partner inflation. For importers, real input costs are higher than the nominal rate suggests. The CNY/PKR rate at 40.95 matters because China is Pakistan's largest import source — if the yuan strengthens, Chinese-origin raw-material costs rise even without a PKR devaluation.</p>

  <p>The <Term k="eff">IMF's</Term> third review praised the SBP's tightening but noted inflation is expected to exceed 10% through Q4 FY26 — IMF language for "be ready to hike again." Reserves at $17.1B provide roughly 4–5 months of import cover; if <Term k="brent">Brent</Term> stays above $105, that coverage shortens.</p>

  <div class="callout-red">
    <strong>So what?</strong>
    The REER signals the rupee's nominal stability obscures real-term overvaluation. If the SBP eventually allows nominal depreciation to correct it, PKR import costs jump. Lock in FX rates on import LCs now, and track CNY/PKR alongside USD/PKR.
  </div>

  <p>Brent rose 8.1% to close at $109.26 — its largest weekly gain since the conflict began. Three catalysts compounded: Washington rejected Iran's counterproposal and called the ceasefire failing; Tehran signalled it is prepared to return to fighting; and reporting emerged that the US and Israel are intensifying strike preparations.</p>

  <p>The Brent–WTI spread narrowed to $3.80 from $6 in Edition 01. That convergence is significant — US domestic inflation fears are now pulling WTI higher alongside Brent, rather than Middle East risk driving Brent alone. The energy shock is no longer regional; it is global. The IEA's most consequential signal: Hormuz crude flows dropped 4M bpd in March–April, and the market could stay "materially undersupplied through October" even under an optimistic resolution. This is a structural supply deficit, not a short-term disruption.</p>

  <div class="callout">
    <strong>Pakistan's import benchmark</strong>
    Dubai Platts rose from $98 Monday to $105 Friday — every day closed above Edition 01's $97.58. At $105, Pakistan's monthly crude import bill runs roughly $240M higher than pre-conflict. Aramco's June <Term k="osp">OSPs</Term> to Asia will reflect this week's elevated levels.
  </div>
