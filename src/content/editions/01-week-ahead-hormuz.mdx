But the stability is being bought with rising real rates: the policy rate rose 100bps to 11.5% on 27 April, and <Term k="cpi">CPI</Term> has returned to double digits at 10.9% year-on-year.</p>

  <p>Oil markets delivered one of the most volatile weeks since the Hormuz crisis began. <Term k="brent">Brent</Term> swung from $116/bbl on Monday to $100/bbl by Friday — a $19 intra-week range — before settling around $101, down roughly 7% on the week.</p>

  <p>Dubai Platts is the benchmark that matters most for Pakistan. It prices the medium-sour crude loading from the Middle East Gulf — the grade Pakistan actually buys. The Brent–WTI spread has widened to ~$6/bbl from a pre-crisis $3, reflecting the disproportionate impact on globally-benchmarked Brent. US crude inventories fell 2.3M barrels; <Term k="backwardation">backwardation</Term> in the futures curve signals continued tight near-term supply.</p>

<section class="section">
  <div class="section-header"><span class="section-num">03</span><h2 class="section-title">Economic Watchout</h2></div>
  <p>The SBP's 100bps hike signals the bank is prioritising the rupee and inflation anchor over growth. With CPI momentum at +2.5% month-on-month — the fastest in nine months — further tightening cannot be ruled out at the June MPC. Plan financing decisions on the assumption that rates stay higher for longer.</p>
</section>

<section class="section">
  <div class="section-header"><span class="section-num">04</span><h2 class="section-title">Commodity Watch</h2></div>
  <p>Palm oil sits in a rare soft patch, but Malaysia's B15 biodiesel mandate begins 1 June — a structural demand pull that may close the current buying window. Packaging resins (HDPE/LLDPE) face constrained Gulf supply; Southeast Asian alternatives from Thailand and Vietnam are worth qualifying now.</p>
</section>

<section class="section">
  <div class="section-header"><span class="section-num">05</span><h2 class="section-title">Logistics & Ports</h2></div>
  <p>Hormuz disruption continues to lengthen lead times on Gulf-origin cargo and raise war-risk insurance premiums. Build buffer into delivery schedules for any input routed through the Strait.</p>
</section>

<section class="section">
  <div class="section-header"><span class="section-num">06</span><h2 class="section-title">Regulatory & Policy</h2></div>
  <p>The fourth fuel-price hike since March feeds directly into transport and input costs. Watch for any compensating relief measures, and price contracts with an escalation clause where possible.</p>
</section>

<section class="section">
  <div class="section-header"><span class="section-num">07</span><h2 class="section-title">Weather & Agriculture</h2></div>
  <p>The PMD has confirmed El Niño is likely from June to September, pointing to a below-normal monsoon and stress on rice and cotton. Begin contingency planning and consider forward contracts with agricultural suppliers.</p>
</section>

<section class="section">
  <div class="section-header"><span class="section-num">08</span><h2 class="section-title">Political & Disruption Risk</h2></div>
  <p>Tehran's response to the US framework — routed through Pakistan — is expected this week and is the single biggest swing factor for oil and freight. Scenario-plan for both a Hormuz reopening and a prolonged closure rather than betting on either.</p>
</section>
