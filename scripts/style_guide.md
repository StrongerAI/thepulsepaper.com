# The Pulse Paper — Writing Style Guide

Extracted from five weekly editions (May 10 – June 8, 2026) and five standalone Signal articles. Use this as the reference document when building draft generators or briefing contributors.

---

## 1. Voice and Register

**Institutional but personal, expert but not academic.** The voice is that of a knowledgeable insider addressing a peer — not a professor lecturing, not a journalist reporting, but a smart colleague who has already done the analysis and is now telling you what it means for your decisions.

The reader is a business decision-maker — typically FMCG procurement, supply chain, or C-suite in Pakistan. Every paragraph ends implicitly in "...so what does this mean for your business?"

**No first person.** No "we," no "I." The voice is institutional but not bureaucratic. Attribution is always direct: "The IEA warned," "Trump stated," "OGRA is considering."

**"You" is used constantly and specifically.** Not a generic investor — a specific business operator: "your LC financing," "your route costs," "your pricing strategy," "your FMCG CFO."

---

## 2. Sentence Structure

**Wide variation in length is defining.** Short declarative sentences alternate with longer analytical ones that build with colons and semicolons. No long passages of uniformly medium-length sentences.

**Short sentences are used for emphasis and editorial verdicts:**
- "This is not a short-term disruption. It is a structural supply deficit."
- "The fear faded. The structural picture didn't."
- "Banks and institutional investors were not gently rotating into government paper. They were scrambling for it."
- "That is the trap."

**Long sentences use colons to pivot to the punchline, or semicolons to join closely related clauses:**
- "The signal from the auction is not that PIB yields have moved below the policy rate; they have not. The signal is that medium-tenor government yields collapsed from the previous auction and now sit only modestly above the policy rate, suggesting investors expect the easing cycle to continue and want duration before the next leg lower."

**"But" at the start of a sentence is used freely** to pivot, as are colons for introducing the key number or the key implication.

---

## 3. How Data Is Introduced

**Numbers almost never lead a sentence cold.** Context first, then number. The number is the landing point of a clause.

Wrong: "$109.26 was where Brent closed Friday."
Right: "Brent rose 8.1% to close at $109.26, its largest weekly gain since the conflict began."

**YoY/MoM comparisons:** Current reading, then the comparison, then the implication.
- "April CPI vaulted to 10.9% from 7.3% — the highest since July 2024."
- "Exports fell 3% year-on-year to US$2.37bn in May, while imports rose 3% to US$5.69bn."

**The delta is always named.** "Rose 8.1% to $109.26," not just "rose to $109.26." "Fell from 13.25% to 12.14%, a drop of 111 basis points" — the change is quantified, not implied.

**Currency conventions in prose:**
- Rupees: "Rs" (not PKR)
- Large rupee figures: Rs 864B (billions), Rs 1.73T (trillions); spelled out in formal constructions
- Dollars: "$3.83B" — no "USD" in running prose
- Percentages: always "%" symbol, not "percent"
- Rates: "Rs 415/L," "$97.58/bbl," "MYR 4,541/MT"
- Basis points: "bps" always, "basis points" only on first use

**Approximations:** "~$104" in data cards; "around $104" or "roughly 100bps" in prose. Never "approximately 104 dollars."

---

## 4. Editorial Judgement — When and How

**The writer commits hard.** Very little hedging. Analytical judgements are stated as fact.
- "This is not a short-term disruption. It is a structural supply deficit."
- "The surplus leaned on transfers, levies and deferrals, not durable structural revenue."
- "The ceasefire trade is priced in, not signed."

**Phrases signalling the shift from fact to interpretation:**
- "The signal is not X. The signal is Y."
- "What this means practically:"
- "That is the whole story in one sentence:"
- "The distinction that matters for planning is..."
- "That is the trap."

**The writer never hedges with:** "it could be argued that," "one might suggest," "perhaps," "it seems," "some analysts believe." When quoting others' uncertainty, it is attributed specifically — "The IMF's baseline assumes..." — then the writer states whether they agree or disagree.

---

## 5. Section Openings

**Lead with the contradiction, the key number, or the editorial verdict. No preamble.**

Type 1 — State the contradiction directly:
> "The nominal USD/PKR rate at 279 suggests stability. But Pakistan's Real Effective Exchange Rate (REER) tells a different story."

Type 2 — Name the key number and immediately annotate its significance:
> "The SBP received Rs1.903tr in total realized bids against a Rs350bn target in the 17 June PIB auction, 5.4 times covered on that basis."

Type 3 — State the editorial verdict, then prove it:
> "Oil markets delivered one of the most volatile weeks since the Hormuz crisis began. Brent swung from $116/bbl on Monday to $100/bbl by Thursday before settling near $101 on Friday — a $16 intra-week range driven entirely by diplomatic signaling."

Sub-section headings are editorial in themselves, not descriptive: "The REER Problem," "The Inflation Headline Is a Trap," "How FY26's Surplus Was Really Built."

---

## 6. Section Closings

**The dominant closing move is a forward-looking prescription, a binary risk statement, or a single sentence that captures the structural truth.**

- "Plan fuel costs off the signature and the levy line, not the weekly notice."
- "Take the relief, but don't bank on it."
- "Two cuts in a row feels like a turning point. It is more likely a pause. The war premium came out. The supply problem is still in."
- "The May data is a good headline. The 11-month trajectory and trade composition are what actually matter."

**Never** end a section by summarising what was just said. Add forward motion.

---

## 7. What the Writer Never Does

- **No passive voice** in analytical sentences. "The IMF warned" not "it was warned by the IMF."
- **No vague attribution.** Never "according to sources" or "experts say." Always: "The IEA estimates," "Trump stated," "OGRA reportedly considering."
- **No filler transitions.** "Furthermore," "Moreover," "It is important to note," "It should be mentioned" — none of these appear. Transitions are either implicit (new paragraph), structural (a sub-heading), or "But" at the start of a sentence.
- **No summary repetition.** Never end a section by recapping what was just said.
- **No hedging adjectives** around editorial calls. "Arguably," "seemingly," "perhaps" are absent.
- **No economic clichés.** "Storm clouds," "perfect storm," "silver lining," "light at the end of the tunnel" — none.
- **No unnecessary precision on estimates.** "~$104" not "$103.97." But when the source is exact (PIB yield of 12.14%, fuel price of Rs 414.58/L), full precision is used.
- **No bullet points in prose paragraphs.** Lists appear only in structured callout boxes or action-item sections.
- **No "it is worth noting."** Just note it.

---

## 8. Recurring Phrases and Constructions

**"This is not X. This is Y."** — Signature move for distinguishing between two things that look similar:
- "This is not a short-term disruption. It is a structural supply deficit."
- "Banks and institutional investors were not gently rotating into government paper. They were scrambling for it."

**"The [signal/number] is not X. The [signal/number] is Y."**:
- "The signal from the auction is not that PIB yields have moved below the policy rate; they have not. The signal is that..."
- "The number headline writers will use is correct. The number worth understanding is US$4.25bn."

**"Watch [specific event/data point]."** — Used to focus attention on a single catalyst.

**"That is the trap."** — Standalone sentence to name a planning error.

**"[Fact]. [Implication]. [Action.]"** — Three-beat structure in callout boxes.

**Edition cross-referencing:** "Edition 01 outlined two scenarios," "↑ from $97.58 Ed.01." The writer treats each edition as a running record, updating earlier calls explicitly.

---

## 9. "What to Watch" Structure

**In weekly editions:** A labelled callout box at the end of major sections, headed "What to Watch," "Diplomacy Watch," "FMCG Distribution Cost Alert," etc.

**In standalone articles:** A `## What to watch` header with bolded sub-items:
- **"For businesses with floating-rate debt:"** [paragraph]
- **"For the government's fiscal position:"** [paragraph]

**Specificity is high.** Dates where known ("Friday 22 May"), specific triggers ("If Brent stays above $95..."), specific consequences ("another Rs 10-15 hike is near-certain"). No vague "watch this space."

**Binary scenarios** name two cases (Bear/Bull), each with a price range, a trigger event, and an explicit action.

---

## 10. Paragraph Rhythm

**Standard paragraph: 3–5 sentences.** Typical structure:
1. The fact or event (what happened)
2. The mechanism or context (why, or what it means)
3. The implication or forward consequence
4. Optional: short landing statement

**Single-sentence paragraphs appear 2–3 times per section** to land editorial verdicts.

**Long paragraphs (6–8 sentences)** appear in executive summaries and data-dense background passages.

**Sub-section paragraphs are shorter (2–3 sentences)** because the heading does the framing work.

---

## 11. Weekly Edition Structure

Ten sections, always in the same order:

| # | Section | Content |
|---|---------|---------|
| 01 | PKR & Macro Pulse | Exchange rate, CPI, SBP rate, REER, reserves. Data cards + 2-3 analytical paragraphs + callout. |
| 02 | Oil Markets & Energy Deep Dive | Crude benchmarks, Dubai Platts, intra-week chart, scenarios, Pakistan fuel prices. Longest section. |
| 03 | Economic Watchout | KSE-100, fiscal/budget news, IMF, global rates. Tables + analysis. |
| 04 | Commodity Watch | CPO, sugar, wheat, resins, etc. Input commodity table + brief analysis. |
| 05 | Logistics & Ports | Hormuz status, shipping, Karachi port, surcharges. Data cards + 2 paragraphs + callout. |
| 06 | Regulatory & Policy | Timeline of SROs, fuel revisions, SBP actions, government decisions. |
| 07 | Weather & Agriculture | Monsoon outlook, water/irrigation, crops. Data cards + 2-3 paragraphs + callout. |
| 08 | Political & Disruption Risk | Geopolitical events + Domestic Disruption Calendar table (trigger, timing, probability, impact). |
| 09 | Global Supply Chain Signals | Summary table of global signals + Pakistan relevance. Minimal prose. |
| 10 | Action Items | Prioritised list with red/amber/green icons. Bolded action + explanatory span. |

**Executive Summary:** Standalone block before the table of contents. 3 paragraphs: (1) what happened vs what was predicted, (2) domestic implications, (3) global through-line. ~400-600 words. Bold lead sentences.

## 12. Numbers and Precision

| Situation | Convention |
|-----------|-----------|
| Exact data (OGRA notification, auction cut-off) | Full precision: Rs 414.58/L, 12.14% |
| Intra-week estimates, ranges | Approximation: ~$104, roughly 100bps |
| Billions (rupees, in callouts) | Rs 864B |
| Trillions (rupees) | Rs 1.73T |
| Billions (dollars) | $3.83B |
| Percentages | Always % symbol |
| Basis points | bps (basis points only on first use) |
| Unit rates | Rs 415/L, $97.58/bbl, MYR 4,541/MT |
| YoY/MoM | Always name the delta: "fell 3% YoY to..." |
